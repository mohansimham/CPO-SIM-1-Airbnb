Before You Begin

A quick refresher on the frameworks you'll apply in this simulation.

🧭

Strategic Thinking

Seeing the big picture before making a move

Strategic thinking is the ability to weigh long-term impact against short-term gains. It means considering second-order effects — not just "what happens next" but "what does this make possible or impossible later?"

How to apply it

When faced with a decision, ask: Does this move expand or shrink our future options? What's the cost of reversing this later? Who benefits in the short term vs. long term?

Example

Should Airbnb expand into hotels? A short-term revenue play might dilute the brand that makes Airbnb unique — the strategic thinker weighs both sides before deciding.

Strategic Decision Framework

  1. Define the decision — What exactly are we choosing between?
  2. Map second-order effects — If we do X, what does that enable or prevent later?
  3. Identify reversibility — Is this a one-way door or a two-way door?
  4. Time horizon test — What's the 1-year, 3-year, 10-year impact?
  5. Opportunity cost — What are we NOT doing by choosing this?
  6. Stakeholder impact map — Who wins, who loses, who's indifferent?
  7. Worst-case scenario — If this fails completely, can we survive it?

Stakeholder Management

Balancing competing interests without losing trust

In a platform business, every decision affects multiple stakeholders — hosts, guests, regulators, investors, employees. Stakeholder management means understanding whose needs to prioritise and when, without alienating the rest.

How to apply it

Map out who is affected by the decision. Ask: Whose trust is hardest to rebuild? Which stakeholder has the most leverage right now? Can I find a solution that partially satisfies multiple groups?

Example

A city threatens to ban Airbnb unless listings are capped. Hosts want freedom, guests want supply, regulators want compliance — the PM must find a path that keeps the platform viable.

Stakeholder Mapping Template

  1. List all affected stakeholders — Internal (eng, sales, legal) + external (users, regulators, partners).
  2. Map Power vs Interest — High power + high interest = manage closely. Low both = monitor.
  3. Identify decision rights — Who has veto power? Who has input rights? Who's informed only?
  4. Define communication cadence — Weekly updates, milestone reviews, or escalation-only?
  5. Surface hidden conflicts — Where do stakeholder goals contradict each other?
  6. Build the DACI — Driver, Approver, Contributors, Informed for each decision.

Risk Mitigation

Anticipating what could go wrong and preparing for it

Risk mitigation isn't about avoiding all risk — it's about identifying the highest-impact risks and having a plan. In product decisions, this means thinking about reputational damage, safety incidents, legal exposure, and platform trust.

How to apply it

For every option, ask: What's the worst-case scenario? How likely is it? What's our recovery plan if it happens? Is the upside worth the downside risk?

Example

A guest reports a safety incident at an Airbnb property. Do you quietly handle it, or publicly change policy? The risk of inaction (more incidents, media exposure) vs. the risk of overreaction (host backlash) both need weighing.

Risk Assessment Matrix

  1. Identify risks — Brainstorm: what could go wrong? Technical, legal, reputational, financial.
  2. Rate likelihood — Low / Medium / High probability of occurring.
  3. Rate impact — Low / Medium / High severity if it does occur.
  4. Plot the matrix — High likelihood + high impact = act now. Low both = accept.
  5. Define mitigation actions — For top risks: prevent, reduce, transfer, or accept?
  6. Set trigger points — At what signal do you activate contingency plans?
  7. Assign owners — Every risk has one person accountable for monitoring it.

⚖️

Product Judgment

Knowing when to scale, experiment, or say no

Product judgment is the hardest skill to teach — it's the ability to make the right call when data is incomplete, stakeholders disagree, and time is short. It combines pattern recognition, empathy, and strategic awareness.

How to apply it

Ask: What does the data say? What doesn't the data capture? What would I advise a friend to do? If this decision were irreversible, would I still make it?

Example

Airbnb Experiences is growing slowly but has passionate users. Do you double down or reallocate resources to the core business? There's no formula — this is where judgment matters.

Decision Quality Checklist

  1. Data check — What does quantitative evidence say? What's the sample size?
  2. Qualitative signal — What are users saying that data doesn't capture?
  3. Conviction vs consensus — Am I agreeing because I believe it or because others do?
  4. Reversibility test — If I'm wrong, can I undo this? What's the cost of reversal?
  5. Pre-mortem — Assume it failed. Why did it fail? What did I miss?
  6. Advisor test — What would I tell a peer in this exact situation?
  7. Ship-or-kill deadline — Set a time limit. Decide by then or default to no.
